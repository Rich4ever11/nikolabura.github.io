`d/dx f(x)`

Up next, we will learn the concept of differentiation. This is, again, one of the most important ideas in calculus.

First, let's review the idea of slope. We'll use a simple linear example function.

How would one calculate the slope of this linear function? It is easy, of course...

  1. You take the total change in x-position. We started at `x=2` and ended at `x=10`. So the total change in x-position is `Deltax = 10 - 2 = 8`.

  2. Then you do the same process to find that the total change in y-position is `Deltay = 4 - 0 = 4`.

  3. Finally, divide the change in y over the change in x.
    `dy/dx = 4/8 = 1/2`. So the slope is `1/2`.

Pay attention to that term, `dy/dx`. This is how slope is represented in calculus.

Now, that was all easy pre-calculus stuff, right? Yes, finding the slope of a linear function is easy. After all, it has the same slope at any point on the line. But what happens when the function is not linear?

Uh-oh.

You can try finding the slope of this curve. Say the change in `x` is from `x=0` to `x=1`, and the change in `y` is from `y=0` to `y=1`...

`dy/dx = 1/1 = 1`

A slope of 1? Ridiculous!

Clearly the function is flat near `x=0`, and much steeper near `x=1.5`. The issue is that this function is not linear. Its slope changes depending on the x-position.

With functions like these, we cannot simply find one single "slope" that works for the entire function. So let's try a different approach.

Earlier, we found the slope on a certain interval, like from `x=0` to `x=1`. This is known as the average slope or average rate of change.

But we want something better. We must find the slope at one specific, single point on the function. Let's do `x=1`.

You can now zoom and pan in the graph below. Find `x=1` and zoom in very, very, very close into it. It is marked with a little red dot.

If you zoomed in close enough, you should notice something about the graph. It became linear.

Or at least that's how it looks. The idea here is that you can indeed get the slope from specific points, because on a small enough scale, the curve appears linear - and you know how to calculate the slope of a linear graph!

Remember the formula for finding an average slope?

`dy/dx = (y_"end" - y_"start")/(x_"end" - x_"start")`

So if we are trying to find the slope of `f(x)` at `x=2`, we could try finding the average slope from `x=1` to `x=3`...

`dy/dx = (f(3) - f(1))/(3 - 1)`

But we have already seen that this often won't work for non-linear functions. So how do we find the slope at one tiny, single, specific point?

Remember: we zoom in.

`dy/dx = (f(2.0001) - f(2))/(2.0001 - 2)`

Effectively, we have "zoomed in" so far into the function that our change in x-position is only `0.001` - a tiny amount. On this scale, the function is probably linear enough to take its slope in the normal way. (Of course, this is just a lesson. In reality you should probably zoom even further in.)

`lim_(Deltax->0) (f(x+Deltax) - f(x))/(Deltax)`

This is the "official" limit-based definition of what we just did. As you can see, the change in `x` approaches zero, just as our change in `x` did when you zoomed in to the graph.

What you just did was differentiation - finding the slope of a function at a given point. This slope is known as the instantaneous slope or instantaneous rate of change because it is taken at one specific instant.

Here's the example function again, but this time, when you move the mouse over any point, it shows you the tangent line for that point.

A tangent line, at the point where it touches the function, has the same slope as the function. Tangent lines are very useful for demonstrating differentiation.

Let's try the "zoom-in" strategy again with a different function.

Find the derivative at the point `(2,1)`. For your courtesy, it has again been marked with a complimentary red dot.

Spoiler Alert: It doesn't work.

At `x=2`, this function never appears linear, no matter how much you zoom in. It always comes to a sharp point. So how do you differentiate this at `x=2`?

Unfortunately, you simply cannot. This function, and similar functions which come to sharp points, are nondifferentiable.

Visually finding these instantaneous slopes on a graph, and targeting a single specific point is all well and good... but now, we will learn how to get a function known as the derivative.

This derivative function, when we give it a target x-value, will return the instantaneous slope at that x-value on the original function.
