`f(x) -> f'(x)`

Earlier, you learned how to find the instantaneous slope of a function at any point. A derivative is a function which, given an x-position, will tell you the slope of the original function at that x-position.

If `f(x)` is the original function, then `f'(x)` (read as "f prime x") is its derivative. So if `f` has a slope of `4` at `x=2`, then `f'(2) = 4`.


Move your mouse along either one of the graphs above. Note how the slope of the tangent line (in the upper graph) always matches the value of the derivative (in the lower graph)...

There are a variety of methods, techniques, and tricks for finding derivatives.

Let's start with a constant function.

`f(x) = 4`

Since the value of this function always remains constant, its slope is always zero. Therefore its derivative is always zero. Well, that was easy.

`f'(x) = 0`

Next we'll consider a linear function.

`f(x) = 4x`

The slope of a linear function is always the same. It is simply the coefficient of `x` - in this case, `4`.

`f'(x) = 4`

Now, something more interesting: a quadratic function.

`f(x) = 4x^2`

Now we must use a special rule known as the power rule. This is used when deriving any polynomials, not just quadratics.

If the function is `ax^p` where `a` and `p` are constants, then...

Its derivative is `(a*p)x^(p-1)`.

You take the exponent of `x` and "bring it down" so it multiplies the coefficient of `x`. Then the exponent of `x` is decremented by one. Let's derive our example.

`f(x) = 4x^2`
`f'(x) = (4*2)x^(2-1)`
`f'(x) = 8x^1`


`f'(x) = 8x`
